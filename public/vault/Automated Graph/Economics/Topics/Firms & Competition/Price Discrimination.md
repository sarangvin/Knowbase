# Price Discrimination

## AI Notes
In a standard monopoly or firm-with-market-power model, the firm charges one price to all buyers. **Price discrimination** is what happens when a firm charges *different prices to different buyers for the same good* — not based on cost differences, but based on differences in willingness to pay. The goal is to capture as much consumer surplus as possible and convert it into profit.

Three degrees, from most to least complete:

- **First-degree (perfect) price discrimination**: the firm charges every buyer exactly their maximum willingness to pay — capturing *all* consumer surplus. Every unit that's worth more to a buyer than its cost gets sold, so output reaches the competitive level and there's no deadweight loss — but all the surplus goes to the seller, not the buyer. Purely theoretical; real markets can't perfectly identify each person's reservation price, but personalised pricing algorithms are an approximation.
- **Second-degree price discrimination**: the firm offers different price-quantity bundles and lets buyers self-select. Examples: bulk discounts (more you buy, lower the per-unit price), airline cabin classes (economy vs. business), software tiers (free/pro/enterprise). The firm can't observe your type directly, so it designs the menu of options to get different buyers to reveal themselves.
- **Third-degree price discrimination**: the firm segments buyers into identifiable groups and charges each group a different price based on estimated elasticity. Examples: student/senior discounts, geographic pricing (same drug priced differently in different countries), peak vs. off-peak fares. The group with more inelastic demand gets charged more — they're less able/willing to substitute away.

For price discrimination to work, three conditions must hold: (1) the firm must have **market power** (can't price-discriminate in perfect competition — you'd just lose all customers); (2) buyers must have **different willingness to pay**; (3) **arbitrage must be prevented** — buyers who got a low price can't resell to buyers who'd be charged a high price (e.g. why prescription drugs are non-transferable, why student tickets often require ID).

Efficiency effects are nuanced: price discrimination often *increases* total output (more buyers served at lower prices) and reduces deadweight loss compared to single-price monopoly, even while redistributing surplus from consumers to the firm.
