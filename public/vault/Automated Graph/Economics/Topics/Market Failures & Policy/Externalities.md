# Externalities

## AI Notes
The standard supply/demand model assumes the only people affected by a transaction are the buyer and seller. An **externality** is a cost or benefit that spills over onto someone who wasn't party to the transaction at all.

- **Negative externality** (e.g. pollution from a factory): the *social* cost of production (private cost + harm to bystanders) exceeds the *private* cost the firm actually pays. Because the firm only weighs its private cost, it produces **more** than the socially optimal quantity — the market supply curve sits below the true "social cost" curve.
- **Positive externality** (e.g. vaccination, education, basic research): the *social* benefit (private benefit + benefit to others) exceeds the *private* benefit the buyer captures. Because buyers only weigh their own benefit, the market **under-produces** relative to the social optimum — the demand curve sits below the true "social benefit" curve.

In both cases, the *market equilibrium quantity* diverges from the *socially efficient quantity*, creating a deadweight loss similar in spirit to [[Tax Incidence & Deadweight Loss]] — except here it's caused by a missing price signal, not a tax.

**Standard remedies** — each tries to make private incentives match social costs/benefits:
- **Pigouvian tax** on negative externalities (e.g. carbon tax): raises the private cost to match the social cost, shrinking output toward the efficient level.
- **Subsidy** for positive externalities (e.g. subsidized vaccines or education): lowers the private cost / raises the private benefit to encourage more consumption/production.
- **Cap-and-trade / tradable permits**: cap total externality-causing activity and let the market find the cheapest way to allocate it.
- **Coasean bargaining**: if property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are low, the affected parties can in principle negotiate a private solution without government intervention (Ronald Coase's insight) — though this breaks down with many diffuse parties (e.g. global pollution).

A useful way to hold this topic: externalities are one of the canonical "market failures" — situations where the invisible hand doesn't lead to an efficient outcome because some cost/benefit isn't reflected in anyone's price. Importantly, governments are not exempt: while government policy often *responds* to market externalities, government actions themselves generate externalities (positive: public R&D, vaccination programs, parks; negative: infrastructure displacing communities, subsidies that amplify pollution). Good policy tries to ensure its own externalities are accounted for, though this doesn't always happen.

## Questions
Q: Are governments an externality? Give examples.

A: Governments themselves aren't an externality — an externality is a *spillover effect* from a transaction, not an actor. But government *actions and policies* absolutely generate externalities, both positive and negative, often on parties who had no say in the decision. In that sense, governments are one of the biggest *producers* of externalities in any economy.

**Negative externalities created by governments:**
- **Highway/infrastructure construction**: a new motorway benefits long-distance drivers (the intended beneficiaries) but imposes noise, pollution, and community severance on residents living alongside it — third parties who bear a real cost from a decision made without their direct consent.
- **Military operations**: wars fought for national security create massive negative externalities on civilian populations, neighbouring countries, and future generations (debt, environmental damage, displacement).
- **Subsidies that encourage overproduction**: if a government subsidises a polluting industry (e.g. coal), it amplifies the industry's existing negative externality — third parties bear more pollution than they would have without the subsidy.
- **Austerity / public service cuts**: reducing a public hospital or school imposes costs on the surrounding community that aren't captured in the government's budget calculation.

**Positive externalities created by governments:**
- **Funding basic research**: government-funded R&D (e.g. DARPA funding that produced the internet, NIH research underpinning drug development) generates knowledge spillovers — private firms and the public benefit far beyond what the government "transacted" with the researchers.
- **Vaccination programs**: publicly funded vaccination drives generate herd immunity — a positive externality for everyone, including those who didn't get vaccinated.
- **Urban parks and green spaces**: benefit the whole neighbourhood, not just direct users, through improved air quality, reduced heat, and higher property values.
- **Education subsidies**: a more educated population generates spillovers — lower crime, higher civic participation, more innovation — beyond the private benefit to the individual student.

The key insight: government actions are often *responses to* market externalities (e.g. taxing pollution, subsidising vaccines), but they simultaneously *create* new externalities of their own. Good policy tries to ensure the externalities it creates are positive, or that the negative ones are weighed against the intended benefit — but this doesn't always happen.
