# Public Goods & Common Resources

## AI Notes
Goods can be classified along two dimensions: whether they are **excludable** (can people be prevented from using them?) and whether they are **rival** (does one person's use reduce availability for others?). This 2×2 grid defines four categories, two of which cause market failure.

| | **Rival** | **Non-rival** |
|---|---|---|
| **Excludable** | Private goods (food, clothing) | Club goods (Netflix, toll roads) |
| **Non-excludable** | Common resources (fish stocks, clean air) | Public goods (national defence, street lighting) |

**Public goods** (non-excludable + non-rival) have two problems for markets:
- **Free-rider problem**: because you can't exclude anyone from benefiting, individuals have no incentive to pay voluntarily — they'll free-ride on others' contributions. Result: private markets underprovide or don't provide at all.
- **Non-rivalry**: one person using a lighthouse doesn't reduce its usefulness to ships — the marginal cost of an additional user is zero, so the efficient price is zero, but then no private firm can recover costs.
This is why public goods are the canonical justification for government provision: national defence, basic research, public health surveillance, street lighting.

**Common resources** (non-excludable + rival) have a different problem:
- **Tragedy of the commons**: because no one can be excluded, individuals overuse the resource — each person captures the full benefit of consuming one more unit but shares the cost of depletion with everyone. Overfishing, groundwater depletion, and traffic congestion are all tragedies of the commons.
- Solutions: government regulation (fishing quotas, emissions caps), property rights assignment (privatise the resource so the owner has incentive to manage it sustainably), or community governance (Elinor Ostrom's research showed communities can self-govern common resources without privatisation or top-down regulation, under the right conditions).

Connection to [[Externalities]]: public goods and common resources are really special cases of externality problems. A public good generates positive externalities for everyone (you can't exclude anyone from the benefit); a common resource generates negative externalities as each user degrades the pool for others. The market failure mechanism is the same — costs or benefits aren't reflected in private prices — but the institutional solutions differ.
