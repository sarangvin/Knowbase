# Tax Incidence & Deadweight Loss

## AI Notes
When a good is taxed, the **statutory** burden (who writes the check to the government) and the **economic** burden (whose real purchasing power actually falls) are usually different. A tax on sellers shifts the supply curve up by the tax amount (or, equivalently for a tax on buyers, the demand curve shifts down) — either way, the *gap* between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive equals the tax, and a new, lower-quantity equilibrium forms in between.

**Incidence** — how that gap splits between buyers paying more and sellers receiving less — depends on relative [[Elasticity]], not on who legally pays:
- The side of the market that is **more inelastic** (less able to change quantity in response to price) bears a **larger share** of the tax burden, because they have fewer good alternatives — they can't easily escape the tax by buying/selling less.
- In the extreme: if demand is perfectly inelastic, buyers bear the entire tax; if supply is perfectly inelastic (e.g. a tax on land, which can't be relocated), sellers bear it all.
- This is why payroll taxes nominally "split" between employer and employee often fall mostly on workers (labor supply tends to be relatively inelastic), regardless of the legal split.

**Deadweight loss (DWL)** is the loss of total surplus (consumer + producer) that isn't transferred to anyone — not to the buyer, seller, or government. It arises because the tax raises the price buyers pay above, and lowers the price sellers receive below, what's needed to support the pre-tax equilibrium quantity — so some mutually beneficial trades (where a buyer's value exceeded a seller's cost) no longer happen. Graphically, DWL is the triangle between the supply and demand curves over the range of quantity that "disappears" due to the tax.

DWL grows roughly with the **square** of the tax rate (doubling a tax roughly quadruples the deadweight loss) — small taxes are relatively efficient, large taxes are disproportionately costly. It also grows with elasticity: the more elastic the curves, the more the quantity shrinks in response to the same tax, so the bigger the DWL. This is the basis for the idea of taxing **inelastic** goods (like land or, controversially, addictive goods) if the goal is to raise revenue with minimal distortion — though that runs into separate equity/fairness considerations.

Connection to [[Externalities]]: a tax on a good with a negative externality (a "Pigouvian tax") can actually *reduce* deadweight loss overall, because the "loss" in the simple market sense is offset by reducing an external cost the simple model doesn't count.
