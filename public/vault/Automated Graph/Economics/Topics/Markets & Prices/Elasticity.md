# Elasticity

## AI Notes
Elasticity measures *responsiveness*: how much quantity changes (in percentage terms) when price (or income, or the price of another good) changes by 1%. It's a ratio of percent changes, which makes it unit-free and comparable across very different goods.

**Price elasticity of demand** = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price. By convention reported as a positive number (the negative sign from the downward-sloping demand curve is dropped).
- **Elastic** (|E| > 1): quantity changes more than proportionally — buyers have good substitutes or the good is a large share of their budget (e.g. restaurant meals, brand-name cereal).
- **Inelastic** (|E| < 1): quantity barely responds — necessities, addictive goods, or things with no close substitutes (e.g. insulin, gasoline in the short run).
- **Unit elastic** (|E| = 1): % change in quantity exactly matches % change in price.

What determines elasticity: availability of substitutes (more substitutes → more elastic), how large a share of the budget the good takes, whether it's a necessity vs. luxury, and the time horizon (demand is usually more elastic in the long run — people have time to adjust habits, find alternatives, or change technology).

**Supply elasticity** works the same way but for producers: how easily/quickly can they ramp production up or down in response to price (depends on spare capacity, how quickly inputs can be acquired, time horizon again).

Why it matters downstream: elasticity is the single variable that determines (1) [[Tax Incidence & Deadweight Loss|tax incidence]] — the side of the market (buyers or sellers) that is *more inelastic* (less able to adjust) bears more of a tax's burden, regardless of who officially pays it; (2) the size of the deadweight loss from a tax — more elastic markets lose more total surplus per dollar of tax; and (3) whether [[Price Controls]] (floors/ceilings) cause large or small shortages/surpluses — the more elastic the curves, the bigger the quantity response to a binding control.

There's also **revenue intuition**: if demand is elastic, raising price *reduces* total revenue (quantity drops more than price rises); if inelastic, raising price *increases* revenue. This is why firms with market power care so much about estimating elasticity for their product.

A notable exception to the downward-sloping demand curve: **Veblen goods** (status/luxury items where a higher price *increases* desirability, e.g. designer brands) and the more theoretical **Giffen goods** (staple foods for very poor households, where a price rise can increase quantity demanded because the income effect dominates). Both show "positive" price elasticity — but they're rare exceptions, not a different kind of elasticity.

## Questions
Q: Is there any item that is reverse elastic, as in the more expensive a product is, the more demand there is for that product?

A: Yes — these are exceptions to the normal downward-sloping demand curve, sometimes described as having "positive" price elasticity of demand. Two recognized categories:

- **Veblen goods**: luxury/status goods (designer handbags, high-end watches, certain wines) where a higher price itself makes the good more desirable as a signal of wealth or exclusivity — part of the good's value *is* its price. Demand can rise as price rises, at least over some range, because the good is partly being bought for status rather than its underlying function.
- **Giffen goods**: a more theoretical case — very basic staple foods for very poor consumers (the classic textbook example is rice or bread during famine-era Ireland/China). If the good takes up a huge share of a poor household's budget, a price *increase* can leave them so much poorer that they buy *more* of the staple (cutting back on more expensive foods) and less of everything else — the income effect overwhelms the substitution effect. Real-world confirmed examples are rare and disputed.

Both are edge cases that violate the "law of demand" assumption baked into the basic [[Supply and Demand]] model — worth knowing they exist, but they're the exception, not the rule. Most goods, including most luxury goods, still follow a normal downward-sloping demand curve.
