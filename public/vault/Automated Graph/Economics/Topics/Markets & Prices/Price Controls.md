# Price Controls

## AI Notes
A price control is a government-imposed limit on how high or low a price can legally go. It only has an effect if it's **binding** — set on the "wrong" side of the equilibrium price.

- **Price ceiling** (max legal price), e.g. rent control: binding only if set *below* the equilibrium price. Creates a **shortage** — quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied at that price, since buyers want more than sellers are willing to provide. Consequences: queues/waiting lists, non-price rationing (who you know, first-come-first-served), and a black market where some buyers pay above the legal price.
- **Price floor** (min legal price), e.g. minimum wage or agricultural price supports: binding only if set *above* the equilibrium price. Creates a **surplus** — quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded (e.g. more people willing to work at the minimum wage than employers want to hire), leading to unemployment/unsold inventory, or a government buying up the surplus.

The **size of the shortage or surplus depends on elasticity** ([[Elasticity]]): the more elastic supply and demand are, the larger the gap between quantity supplied and quantity demanded at a given controlled price, because both sides respond more strongly to the price being away from equilibrium. In the short run (more inelastic), a price control may look like it has small effects; over time, as elasticity increases, the gap widens (e.g. rent control's housing shortage tends to worsen over years as fewer units get built or maintained).

Price controls also create **deadweight loss** ([[Tax Incidence & Deadweight Loss]]) — transactions that would have been mutually beneficial at the market price don't happen, because the binding constraint prevents the market from reaching the efficient quantity. Who benefits and who loses isn't uniform: with a ceiling, the buyers who *do* get the good benefit (lower price), but some buyers who would have bought at the market price are now rationed out entirely.

A subtlety: controls are sometimes paired with rationing systems or are partial (e.g. rent control that only applies to some units), which changes the analysis — but the binding-constraint logic is the same starting point for all of these.
